# Recruitment and Screening

Recruitment is the most under-respected phase of qual. A perfectly designed study with perfect moderation cannot recover from the wrong people in the room. Most qual reports that disappoint a client disappoint because the sample was wrong, not because the analysis was weak.

This file covers defining the population, building a sample grid, writing a screener, working with recruiting partners, designing incentives, and handling no-shows and replacements.

---

## Step 1: Define the Population Precisely

Before writing a screener, write a one-sentence definition of who you are trying to talk to. The definition should pass the "can a third party recruit this with no further questions" test.

Weak definitions:

- "Our customers"
- "Small business owners"
- "Decision-makers"
- "Millennials"
- "Frequent users"

Strong definitions:

- "Owners or managers of independent restaurants with 1 to 3 locations, annual revenue between $500K and $3M, who personally chose their current POS system in the past 24 months and were involved in the contract decision"
- "Canadian renters aged 25 to 40 living in Toronto, Vancouver, or Montreal, currently in a rental property they have lived in for at least 6 months, who do not have tenant insurance and have considered but not purchased it in the past 12 months"
- "Hospital-employed cardiologists working in tertiary cardiac care centres in the US, performing at least 3 transcatheter aortic valve replacements per month, with at least 5 years of post-fellowship experience"

The work of writing this sentence is the work of designing the sample. Push back on a brief that does not yet contain it.

A few specific population-definition pitfalls:

- **"Decision-maker" is rarely a single person.** In B2B, decisions involve buyers, users, approvers, blockers, and influencers. Decide which role(s) you are recruiting and write a sentence for each.
- **Self-described categories are not categories.** "Heavy user" means whatever a person wants it to mean. Translate every self-description into a behavioral or factual criterion ("uses the product at least 3 times per week" rather than "heavy user").
- **Lifestyle and psychographic labels do not screen.** "Health-conscious," "early adopter," "tech-savvy" are useless in a recruitment context. Translate to behavior.

## Step 2: Build the Sample Grid

When the study has more than one segment, build a grid. The grid is the design artifact that shows how the recruit will be distributed.

A simple example for a focus group study:

| | New customers (0 to 6 months) | Mature customers (12+ months) |
|---|---|---|
| Direct channel | Group A (n=10) | Group B (n=10) |
| Reseller channel | Group C (n=10) | Group D (n=10) |

A more elaborate example for B2B IDIs:

| Role | Mid-market (100 to 500 employees) | Enterprise (500+ employees) |
|---|---|---|
| Technical buyer (IT director / lead architect) | 4 IDIs | 4 IDIs |
| Business buyer (CFO / VP Ops) | 3 IDIs | 3 IDIs |
| End user (analyst / engineer) | 2 IDIs | 2 IDIs |

The grid forces explicit choices and surfaces gaps. If the grid shows only 2 end users in mid-market, ask: is that enough to learn anything meaningful, or are we wasting four interviews that should be consolidated into a single segment?

For very small populations (specialist B2B), the grid is often a list of named accounts and named roles instead of segments. That is fine. Write the list down.

## Step 3: Write the Screener

A screener is a short questionnaire used to confirm that a potential participant fits the target definition. It is administered by a recruiter, a panel platform, or self-completed online.

A good screener:

- **Is short.** Five to ten questions. Long screeners produce satisficing and dropout.
- **Uses factual questions, not opinions.** Behavior in the past N months. Job title and role. Demographic facts. Brand or product use.
- **Mixes the disqualifying with the necessary.** Disqualify market researchers, journalists, people who work in the category, people who have participated in a study in the past 6 months. Confirm the criteria from your population sentence.
- **Hides the topic.** If respondents know the study is about Brand X, biased respondents will fake their way in.
- **Hides the right answers.** Never expose which answer wins. If asking which brand they use, list 5 to 8 brands plus "none" and "other" so the target brand is not obviously the right one.
- **Is testable.** Before sending to a recruiter, take the screener yourself and verify you would be invited or rejected as expected.

### Screener question types

**Behavioral filter (the workhorse).**

> In the past 12 months, have you personally [done X]?
> - Yes
> - No

> How many [times / units / events] in the past [period]?
> - [Numeric range]

**Role / category filter.**

> Which of the following best describes your role at your company? [Show a list of 6 to 10 options including the target and several adjacent roles.]

**Industry exclusion.**

> Do you or any member of your immediate household work in any of the following industries? Check all that apply.
> - Advertising or marketing
> - Marketing research
> - Journalism / media
> - [The category being studied]
> - None of the above

If any of the first four are checked, terminate.

**Recent participation.**

> Have you participated in a market research study (online survey, focus group, interview) in the past 6 months?

Terminate yes.

**Articulateness check.**

For high-engagement studies, include one short open-ended question to test whether the candidate can express a thought.

> Tell us in a few sentences what is important to you when you are choosing [category].

A response of "I dunno" or three words is a soft fail. A response with two clear sentences is a soft pass.

**Demographic confirmation.**

Age, gender, region, income (if needed). Place these at the end. Many people drop out at demographics, so do not waste them at the top.

### Worked example: a screener for a small-business onboarding study

Population sentence: Owner-operators of US-based small businesses (1 to 10 employees) who personally signed up for a [payments processor X] account in the past 90 days and process at least one transaction per week.

1. Are you the owner or co-owner of a business in the US?
2. How many employees does your business have, including yourself?
3. In which industry does your business operate? [List]
4. Do you currently use [payments processor X] to accept payments?
5. Approximately when did you create your [payments processor X] account?
6. How frequently does your business process payments through [payments processor X] in a typical week?
7. Do you, or anyone in your household, work in any of the following industries? [Marketing research, advertising, payments processing, banking, journalism]
8. Have you participated in a market research study in the past 6 months?
9. In a few sentences, tell us what mattered most to you when choosing how to accept payments for your business.
10. Demographics: age, gender, region.

Quota rules: at least 4 from retail, 3 from food service, 3 from professional services; at least 6 with employees, 6 sole proprietors; mix of recent (under 30 days) and more established (60 to 90 days).

## Step 4: Recruit Wisely

There are four main recruitment channels:

1. **Specialist recruitment partners.** Local facility-based recruiters, national qualitative-recruitment houses (e.g., Schlesinger, Fieldwork, L&E Research, Civicom). Best for general consumer and most B2B work. Cost: $75 to $250 per consumer recruit, $200 to $800 per B2B recruit.

2. **Client lists.** Recruit from the client's customer list. Cheaper but biased (clients tend to keep contact with happier or more engaged customers). Use only when the study explicitly requires customer-of-X participants. Even then, recruit a portion from outside the client list to check for bias.

3. **Panel-based qualitative recruiting.** Online panels (Prolific, User Interviews, Respondent.io, Pollfish) for B2B and consumer. Best for online IDIs and online focus groups. Cost: $50 to $300 per recruit. Risk: more professional respondents.

4. **Networked / snowball recruiting.** For specialist B2B (e.g., physicians performing a rare procedure, founders of a specific kind of startup). Slow but produces high-quality, specifically-defined samples.

For most consumer projects, a specialist recruiter is the right choice. For most B2B online IDIs, a panel like User Interviews or Respondent.io is the right choice. For executive or specialist B2B, networked or list-based recruiting is often the only option.

### Working with a recruiter

A recruiter is your partner. Brief them properly.

- Share the screener, the sample grid, and the field dates.
- Explain why each screening criterion matters. Recruiters fill harder when they understand the why.
- Set a no-show buffer: over-recruit 20 to 30 percent for focus groups, 10 to 15 percent for IDIs.
- Agree on quality controls: how many calls between confirmation and session, how the recruiter verifies identity, whether the recruiter does a quality check on the open-ended question.
- Define replacement rules: if a participant cancels day-of, who finds the replacement and what fee.

A recruiter will rarely turn back on bad respondents on their own. Build that quality check into the process.

## Step 5: Handle Incentives

Incentives are payment for participation. They are not bribes. The principle is: pay enough that participation is worth the participant's time, but not so much that it distorts the sample (induces people who would not otherwise qualify to fake their way in).

Reasonable ranges (2024 to 2026, US/Canada):

| Audience | 60- to 90-min session |
|---|---|
| General consumer | $100 to $150 |
| Hard-to-reach consumer (high-income, niche behavior) | $150 to $250 |
| Small business owner | $150 to $300 |
| B2B individual contributor / manager | $150 to $300 |
| B2B director / VP | $300 to $500 |
| C-suite / specialist (physicians, lawyers, executives) | $500 to $1,500 |
| Online community (1 to 2 weeks of light activity) | $75 to $200 |
| Diary / mobile ethnography (1 to 2 weeks) | $100 to $300 |

Adjust for region and category norms. In some specialist B2B (e.g., transplant surgeons), $1,500 is low. In some consumer studies, $50 is enough.

Pay promptly. Pay through a method participants find easy (PayPal, Tremendous, Visa gift card). Trust is built or broken on payment timing.

A note on B2B and ethics: in some industries (notably healthcare, government, pharmacy), incentive caps and disclosure rules apply. Check the industry-specific rules before fielding.

## Step 6: Plan for No-Shows and Replacements

Focus groups: expect 2 to 4 no-shows per recruited group of 10 to 12. Over-recruit accordingly.

- **Offline focus group:** recruit 10 to 12 to aim for 8 to 10 to show.
- **Online focus group:** recruit 8 to 10 to aim for 6 to 8 to show. No-show and last-minute drop rates run higher online (15 to 30 percent) than offline.
- **Mini-group (offline or online):** recruit 6 to 8 to aim for 4 to 6 to show.

If you want a specific number to attend (say, exactly 5), the screener-side rule of thumb is: recruit 1.5x to 2x your target attendance for online groups, and 1.2x to 1.5x for offline.

IDIs: expect a 5 to 15 percent no-show rate. For a study of 8 IDIs, recruit 9 or 10 with one or two floaters identified who can be moved up if a slot opens.

Reduce no-shows by:

- Confirming twice: at recruitment, and 24 to 48 hours before.
- Sending a clear calendar invite with the join link.
- Reminding the day-of by SMS where possible.
- Building floater slots into the schedule (one or two pre-confirmed participants who are available to sub in).

Replacement rules: when someone cancels, replace within the same segment. Do not relax the screener to fill a slot. An imperfect sample is worse than a smaller sample.

## Common Recruiting Mistakes Beginners Make

1. **Treating recruitment as a checklist.** "We got 10 people who said yes" is not the same as "we got 10 people who fit the sample." Verify, do not assume.

2. **Letting the client recruit their own customers.** Client-recruited samples skew positive, articulate, and senior. Recruit independently or at least split the sample.

3. **Hiding lifestyle questions in the screener.** "Are you health-conscious?" cannot be answered honestly by someone who wants to qualify.

4. **Over-recruiting on too many criteria.** A screener with 20 criteria will find 3 people in the country. Prioritize the must-haves.

5. **Failing to verify identity.** For high-incentive studies, online especially, professional respondents will use multiple identities. Use ID verification or video check-ins at the start of the session.

6. **Recruiting the same people twice.** Same-period repeat respondents bring bias. A 6-month exclusion window is the norm.

7. **Not building in time.** Recruitment takes 2 to 4 weeks for typical consumer work, 4 to 8 weeks for B2B, longer for specialist. Build the timeline backward from field dates.

8. **Penny-wise on the screener.** A weak screener is expensive in disappointing data. Pay for a good screener and a good recruiter.
