# Mode Specifics

The four most common qualitative modes are online IDI, offline IDI, online focus group, and offline focus group. Each has its own logistics, craft, and trade-offs. A fifth mode, asynchronous online community / mobile ethnography, is increasingly common and worth understanding even if you have never run one.

This file covers each mode in turn: what it is good for, what it is bad for, how to set it up, what to watch out for, and what platform options exist.

---

## Offline IDI

A one-on-one interview conducted in person. Settings vary: a participant's home (in-home ethnography-adjacent work), a participant's office (B2B customer visits), a research facility, a neutral location like a coffee shop or hotel meeting room, or a mall (intercept-recruited).

**Strongest for:**

- Sensitive topics where the participant needs full confidentiality
- In-context observation (how a kitchen actually works, how a workshop is laid out)
- Sensory or physical products that need to be touched and used
- High-status professionals on their own turf
- Deep emotional or unconscious material
- B2B customer visits where the work environment is itself the data

**Weakest for:**

- Distributed populations (cost and travel time)
- Fast turnarounds
- Tight budgets
- Topics where the home setting feels invasive or biases the response

**Setup checklist:**

- Confirm location 24 hours before, with full address and contact phone.
- Arrive 10 to 15 minutes early to check the space (lighting, seating, background noise) and set up recording.
- Bring two recorders: a primary (audio interview recorder or phone) and a backup. Test before the participant sits down.
- Prepare a printed consent form. Sign two copies, one for the participant, one for the file.
- Bring printed stimuli even if you plan to show them on a laptop, in case the laptop fails.
- Pack a small kit: notebook, pen, water for both, printed guide, incentive.
- In-home: small gesture of respect (taking off shoes if culturally appropriate, accepting offered tea, complimenting the space without performing).
- Customer visit (B2B): walk-through if offered. Notice the artifacts in the space. Spaces are data.

**Special note: B2B customer visits.** A customer visit is an IDI on the customer's premises, often with a 2-person research team (a moderator and an observer or note-taker from the client side). Typical pattern: 12 to 36 visits worldwide on a single project, 60 to 120 minutes each, structured guide with room for tours and live demos. Schedule a 30-minute team debrief after each visit while it is fresh. McQuarrie's *Customer Visits* book is the canonical reference.

## Online IDI (Video)

A one-on-one interview conducted over Zoom, Teams, Google Meet, or a specialist research platform.

**Strongest for:**

- Distributed populations (cross-country, cross-continent)
- Hard-to-schedule professionals (executives, physicians, working parents)
- Lower-cost projects
- Stimuli that can be screen-shared (digital concepts, websites, prototypes)
- Recording and transcription convenience
- Showing screen sharing of the participant's own environment (showing me how you use the app)

**Weakest for:**

- Physical products that need handling
- Body language reading (much is lost in a head-and-shoulders view)
- Low-trust populations who feel surveilled on camera
- Topics that need a sense of private space
- Participants without strong tech (rural, older, lower-income)

**Setup checklist:**

- Test your platform setup 5 minutes before. Audio, video, screen-share, recording.
- Use a paid platform with cloud recording (Zoom Pro, Teams, or a specialist tool like Discuss.io or Recollective). Free tools have time limits and weaker recording controls.
- Send the join link plus a clear instruction in advance: "Please join from a quiet room with a working camera and microphone. Headphones are helpful."
- Use a clean background or a deliberate blur. Distracting backgrounds (busy bookshelf, moving fan) pull attention from the conversation.
- Look at the camera lens when conveying eye contact. Most monitors are below the camera, so you appear to look down when reading the screen. Practice looking at the lens during the key moments.
- Confirm recording verbally at the start, in addition to any pre-session checkbox.
- Have a phone backup line for IDI participants in case of tech failure. Confirm participant's phone number at the start.
- Brief participants if they will need to use screen share to walk you through something.

**Platform options:**

- **Zoom (Pro):** the workhorse. Cloud recording, breakout rooms, chat, screen share. Most participants know it.
- **Microsoft Teams:** common in corporate B2B. Worse breakout-room and observer-room support than Zoom, but the right pick if the participant's IT department only allows Teams.
- **Google Meet:** simple, fewer features. Fine for short interviews.
- **Discuss.io, Recollective (live), Liveminds, Itracks:** specialist qual platforms with observer rooms, marked-up recording, integrated chat backchannel for client observers. Higher cost but worth it for client-facing projects with multiple observers.
- **Microsoft Teams / Webex:** common in regulated industries (financial services, healthcare) due to enterprise compliance.

**Online IDI craft tips:**

- **Energy management.** A 90-minute video call is more tiring than a 90-minute in-person session. Build in a short pause or stretch break around the 50-minute mark.
- **Probe gaze.** Watch where the participant's eyes go. A participant who keeps glancing off-camera may be checking another screen or losing focus.
- **Silence works the same.** A 3- to 5-second pause after a participant finishes still produces the second-thought. Resist the urge to fill faster online than you would offline.
- **Screen share for stimuli.** Have files ready and open in the right order. Practice the share sequence.
- **Mute and unmute.** Establish at the start that the participant stays unmuted. Background noise is mostly fine. Muting separates participants from the conversation.

## Offline Focus Group

A group of 8 to 12 participants (or 4 to 6 in mini-groups) led by a moderator in a research facility with a one-way mirror and viewing room. Lasts 90 to 120 minutes.

**Strongest for:**

- Group energy and synergy (one comment sparks another)
- Polarization (the fault lines between subsegments emerge in real time)
- Stimulus reactions where group conversation is part of the data
- Exposing internal stakeholders to live customers via the viewing room
- Hearing the natural language people use about the category
- Topics that benefit from disagreement and debate

**Weakest for:**

- Sensitive or socially difficult topics (group inhibits honesty)
- B2B with executives or competitors in the same room
- Detailed individual decisions (the group dilutes depth per person)
- Studies that need to hear from each participant in depth
- Geographies and segments that cannot easily assemble at one place

**Setup checklist:**

- Confirm facility booking 1 week and 24 hours in advance. Verify the setup: seating, recording equipment, viewing room, refreshments.
- Plan the participant arrival flow: greeter at the door, check-in, light refreshments, brief social time before the session starts.
- Brief the host or facility staff on the project (which group is which segment, when each starts, where observers go).
- Seating: round or oval table, moderator at the head. Place dominant or talkative-looking participants away from the moderator; place quieter-looking participants closer.
- Place name cards at each seat (first name only). They help the moderator address people by name.
- Confirm the recording: audio and video, ideally with each participant individually mic'd. Test before the participants arrive.
- Brief observers in the viewing room before participants arrive. Ground rules: silent, no shadows on the mirror, no commentary the moderator can hear, save reactions for the debrief.
- Bring a printed guide for the moderator, printed stimuli, a backup digital copy of stimuli if a display is used.

**Group dynamics specifics:**

- 8 to 12 is the standard size for full offline groups. 4 to 6 is the standard for mini-groups. Smaller groups produce more depth per participant; larger groups produce more language and more polarization.
- Always over-recruit. For a full offline group where you want 8 to 10 to show, recruit 10 to 12. For a mini-group where you want 4 to 6 to show, recruit 6 to 8.
- Homogeneity within group, heterogeneity across groups. If you want to compare two segments, do two groups, not one mixed group.
- Always run more than one group per segment. A single group can be unrepresentative for many reasons (a charismatic dominator, a quiet group, an off day).
- Schedule a 30-minute gap between groups so the moderator can debrief, the facility can reset, and the recording can be saved.

**Beware the viewing room.** Observers in the back room often react in real time, get into side conversations, and form opinions that calcify into "what we heard tonight." Brief them. Also remind them that the few participants they happened to see say more about the recruitment and the room than about the population.

## Online Focus Group (Video)

A synchronous video group of 4 to 6 participants conducted on a video platform with observers in a separate room or link. Typically 60 to 90 minutes.

**Strongest for:**

- Distributed samples (a single group can include participants from multiple cities)
- Niche or hard-to-assemble populations
- Faster fielding (no travel, no facility booking)
- Lower cost per group
- Topics where physical co-presence is not essential

**Weakest for:**

- Stimulus interaction with physical objects
- Energy-driven topics where the room dynamic is the point
- Long sessions (90+ minutes is hard online)
- Populations with weak tech access

**Setup checklist:**

- **Recruit 8 to 10, aim for 6 to 8 to show up.** This is the most important number to get right. No-show and last-minute cancellation rates are high on video (15 to 30 percent), so over-recruit deliberately. A group that arrives at 6 to 8 attending is manageable on video; a group that arrives at 4 is unusable for group dynamics; a group that arrives at 10 is chaos. The recruit-to-show buffer is the discipline.
- Shorter is better. 60 to 75 minutes is the workable length. 90 minutes is the absolute ceiling.
- Use a specialist qual platform (Discuss.io, Recollective live, Itracks, Liveminds) or a Zoom Pro with deliberate setup: gallery view, mute discipline, observer link.
- Brief participants in advance on platform setup. Provide a 1-page guide and an optional pre-call.
- Have a co-moderator or tech support person available to handle drop-outs, audio issues, and chat moderation. This is a hard role to do alone online.
- Use chat for shy participants, for quick votes ("type a 1 to 5 in chat"), and as a parallel channel without disrupting the audio conversation.
- Brief observers in a separate room or chat. The platform should support a one-way observer experience.
- Build in tech buffer time: 5 minutes for late arrivals and setup at the start, 5 minutes for closing without rushing.

**Online focus group craft tips:**

- Set the mute rule clearly: unmuted unless noisy.
- Round-robins on each major question. Online does not have the natural conversational turn-taking of a physical room. Without explicit invitations to speak, the same one or two people dominate.
- Silent writing exercises work well online. Give each participant 90 seconds to type their answer in a Google Doc or chat, then go around the screen to discuss.
- Stimuli via screen share. Pre-load files, practice the order.
- Use the "spotlight" or "pin" features when one participant is sharing or showing something.
- Watch the chat. Sometimes the quietest voice in audio is the loudest in chat.

## Asynchronous Online Community / MROC / Mobile Ethnography

A research format where participants post text, photos, or short videos over hours, days, or weeks. The moderator interacts with posts, asks follow-up questions, and assigns daily or weekly activities.

**Strongest for:**

- In-the-moment context (in-store experiences, in-app frustrations, weekly cooking)
- Customer journeys over time
- Iterative concept exploration (post a concept, gather reactions, refine, post next day)
- Sensitive topics where reflective written responses are more honest than live conversation
- Distributed populations with no overlap in schedule
- Building a corpus of customer language for downstream survey design

**Weakest for:**

- Tight deadlines (these studies take 1 to 4 weeks minimum)
- Topics requiring deep one-on-one moderator probing
- Populations with low literacy or low platform familiarity
- Studies where you need rich group debate (asynchronous is parallel, not interactive)

**Setup checklist:**

- Pick a platform: Recollective (general-purpose), dscout (mobile-first), Indeemo (mobile/visual), Discuss.io, FocusVision Decipher.
- Design the activity schedule: typically 1 to 3 short activities per day for a week, or a small number of larger activities over 2 to 4 weeks.
- Mix the activity types: writing prompts, photo/video diary entries, polls, peer reactions to others' posts.
- Brief participants on what is expected: time commitment per day, what good participation looks like, what the incentive structure is.
- Tier the incentive by participation: a higher payment for active, consistent participants; reduce or remove for low-effort responses.
- Plan moderator engagement: someone reads and responds to posts within 12 to 24 hours. This is the most labor-intensive part of running an MROC.

**MROC craft tips:**

- **Treat the first day as onboarding.** Have a low-stakes intro activity ("post a photo of where you are right now and tell us where you are sitting"). This gets every participant familiar with the platform.
- **Don't over-prompt.** A pile of daily activities will burn out participants by day 3. Two short, well-designed activities per day is the working ceiling.
- **Probe individually.** Comment on individual posts with probes. "Tell me more about this." "What was happening at that moment?" Treat each thread as a mini-IDI.
- **Build a thread of insights as you go.** Asynchronous studies produce data continuously. Capture themes daily, not just at the end.

## Choosing the Mode for the Question

A short decision tree:

1. **Does the topic need real-time group interaction (polarization, energy, group synergy)?** If yes, focus group (offline if budget and geography allow, online if not). If no, IDI or asynchronous.

2. **Does the topic need in-context behavioral observation (homes, factories, stores)?** If yes, offline IDI or asynchronous mobile diary. If no, online IDI is the default.

3. **Is the population distributed across geographies?** If yes, lean online (IDI or focus group) or asynchronous.

4. **Is the topic sensitive or socially difficult?** If yes, IDI (offline preferred where rapport matters most). Avoid focus groups for shame, finances, sexuality, illness, etc.

5. **Is the timeline 2 weeks or less?** Online live (IDI or focus group). Asynchronous and offline both need longer ramp.

6. **Is the budget tight?** Online live; consider an online community for richer data per dollar.

7. **Does the client team need to see and feel customers directly?** Offline focus group with viewing room, or online focus group with a live observer link.

Combinations are common and often better. An online community to build context plus a few live IDIs or groups to deepen on the most interesting threads is a frequent strong design.

## Common Mode-Choice Mistakes

1. **Defaulting to focus groups.** Focus groups are the most familiar format to non-researchers. They are not always the best choice. Re-decide for each project.

2. **Recruiting too few for an online focus group.** Online no-show and last-minute drop rates are 15 to 30 percent. Recruiting 5 people for a group of 5 means many groups end up with 3 attending, which is too few for real group dynamics. Recruit 8 to 10 to aim for 6 to 8 attending.

3. **Long online sessions.** 90 minutes online is hard for everyone. Cut to 75 minutes when possible.

4. **Running an offline group in a region without a viewing room.** Without a viewing room, much of the value of the offline group (stakeholder learning) is lost. Reconsider; consider streaming the session online for the client team.

5. **Asynchronous studies with no moderator engagement.** Participants disengage fast if no one is reading and responding to their posts. The moderator is doing work every day, not just at the end.

6. **Mobile ethnography without a written guide for participants.** Participants need clear instructions on what to record, when, and why. "Take a photo of your kitchen counter when you finish making dinner" is clear. "Document your kitchen routines" is not.

7. **Mixing modes without a reason.** Combinations are good when each mode serves a distinct purpose. Combinations for the sake of looking thorough waste budget.
