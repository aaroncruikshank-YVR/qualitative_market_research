# Research Design

This file covers the design decisions that come before recruitment: deciding whether qual is the right tool at all, and if so, which qual method (IDI, focus group, customer visit, online community, or a combination) best fits the question and the conditions.

A research project that gets design wrong cannot be saved by good fieldwork. A weak guide moderated well still produces shallow findings. A great moderator running the wrong method still answers the wrong question. Spend disproportionate effort here.

---

## When to Choose Qualitative Research

The single best test: **do we already know what to count?**

If yes, run a survey. You have the categories, the language, the response options. You need to know how prevalent each is and how they relate. Quant is the right tool.

If no, you do not yet have the categories. You do not yet know the right words. You do not yet know what the meaningful variation looks like. Qual is the right tool: it surfaces what exists, in what forms, and why it might matter.

A second useful test: **is this finding going to land as a number or as a story?** Numbers are for stakeholders who need to size, decide between, or track. Stories are for stakeholders who need to empathize, generate ideas, or interpret a surprising result.

Qual is the right call when:

1. **The problem is poorly defined.** You do not yet know what variables matter. Qual maps the territory before you draw the map.
2. **The topic is sensitive, embarrassing, or socially loaded.** People will not give honest answers on a closed survey item about a topic they are ashamed of, conflicted about, or have not thought through. Qual creates space for nuance and contradiction.
3. **Motivations are unconscious or subterranean.** People often do not know why they buy what they buy. Qual techniques (laddering, projectives, observation) can reach material that direct questioning cannot.
4. **You need the participants' own vocabulary.** Before writing closed-end survey items, you need to know what words people use, what categories they see, how they describe the experience.
5. **You are interpreting a quant result.** A survey shows that satisfaction dropped 8 points. The CSAT verbatims hint at "the app" but you do not know what about the app. Qual interviews unpack the mechanism.
6. **The population is small or unreachable through scale.** Surgeons performing a rare procedure. Buyers of an enterprise platform. CEOs of mid-cap manufacturers. Sample sizes that work for surveys simply do not exist in these populations.
7. **You need behavioral or contextual data.** What people do in their kitchen, on their factory floor, during onboarding, can only be observed (or self-reported in context). Survey self-reports of behavior are notoriously unreliable.

Qual is the wrong call when:

- A stakeholder needs a population estimate ("what share of customers prefer A over B"). Quant.
- The decision is high-stakes and requires precise sizing. Quant or qual-then-quant.
- The category is well-understood, mature, and tracked. Quant tracking with periodic qual deep-dives.
- The deadline is too short to recruit properly. Better to delay than to run rushed qual with the wrong sample.

When in doubt, run a **pluralistic design**: qual first to map and define, then quant to size and confirm. Or quant first to flag the surprising result, then qual to explain it. The two methods answer different questions and combine well.

## IDI vs. Focus Group

The in-depth interview (IDI) and the focus group are the two workhorses of commercial qual. They have different strengths and beginners often pick the wrong one out of habit (focus groups are familiar; clients love viewing rooms; therefore everyone gets a focus group).

Use the table to decide:

| Condition | IDI | Focus group |
|---|---|---|
| Topic is sensitive, embarrassing, or competitive | Strong | Weak (group inhibits) |
| You need detail on an individual decision | Strong (full hour on one person) | Weak (split across 8 people) |
| You want to hear how people talk to each other | Weak (no group) | Strong (group is the point) |
| You want polarization to surface | Weak | Strong (groups expose fault lines) |
| You need to test stimuli with clean reactions | Mixed (clean but isolated) | Strong if reactions are public; weak if private |
| Participants are professional / executive / hard to schedule | Strong (their time, their place) | Very weak (impossible to assemble) |
| You want to expose internal stakeholders to live customers | Weak (one client per IDI is a lot) | Strong (viewing room scales) |
| You need participants' own vocabulary | Mixed | Strong (group conversation produces vocabulary fast) |
| Budget is tight | Mixed (depends on number of IDIs) | Strong per insight (groups concentrate participants) |
| Time is tight | Mixed | Strong (a few groups can cover an audience) |
| You need to surface lots of distinct ideas | Strong (research shows IDIs out-produce groups on idea count) | Weak |

A useful rule of thumb: **focus groups are dramatic and memorable but selectively reveal**. **IDIs are private and slow but reveal cumulatively**. If the buyer values energy and the live event, a group will land harder. If the buyer values depth and considered understanding, IDIs will land harder.

A third option, often overlooked: **mini-groups of 4 to 6**. They keep some group dynamic but are easier to recruit, give each participant more airtime, and reduce the dominator problem. Good middle ground for harder topics that still benefit from some group interaction.

A fourth option: the **customer visit**. This is an IDI conducted at the customer's place of business, often by a small cross-functional team (a researcher plus one or two from the client). It is the workhorse of B2B qual: 12 to 36 visits worldwide, two-hour conversations on the customer's turf, with an immediate team debrief after each. Customer visits sit inside the IDI family but have their own logistics (covered in `mode-specifics.md`).

## Mode Choice: Online vs. Offline

Once you have decided IDI or group, the next choice is mode. This is no longer a simple online/offline binary. Several modes are in play:

- **Offline IDI.** In a home, office, or facility. Strongest behavioral signal.
- **Online IDI by video.** Zoom, Teams, Webex. The default for most contemporary commercial qual.
- **Telephone IDI.** Still useful for B2B and for sensitive topics where video feels invasive. Lower fidelity but lower friction.
- **Offline focus group.** In a facility with one-way mirror and viewing room.
- **Online focus group by video.** Smaller (4 to 6), shorter, on a platform like Discuss.io, Recollective live, or a vanilla Zoom with a moderator and observer link.
- **Online research community (MROC).** Asynchronous, days or weeks long, 25 to 100 participants posting text, photos, and short videos.
- **Mobile ethnography / video diaries.** Asynchronous, individual. Participants self-record over time. Tools include dscout, Indeemo, Recollective.

Trade-offs are in `mode-specifics.md`. The short version: online costs less and reaches further; offline produces richer behavioral and emotional signal; asynchronous formats capture in-the-moment context that no live session can replicate. Many strong projects combine modes (e.g., one online community for context, three offline groups for live concept testing).

## Writing Research Objectives

A qual project needs a tight set of objectives that drive every downstream choice: who to recruit, what to ask, how to analyze.

A good qual research objective is:

- **Decision-oriented.** It is anchored to a decision someone will make based on the findings. If no decision depends on the answer, the project is a fishing trip.
- **Bounded.** It names a population, a behavior or experience, and a time frame.
- **Open-ended.** It asks "what" or "how" or "why," not "how many."

Worked examples:

| Weak objective | Strong objective |
|---|---|
| Understand our customers | Understand how mid-market IT directors evaluate observability platforms during the 6 to 8 weeks before a purchase decision |
| Test our new ad | Explore how 25- to 40-year-old urban Canadian renters react to two versions of a tenant-insurance ad, including what they see as the main message, what they trust and distrust, and what they would tell a friend about each |
| Find out why people are leaving | Understand the journey, decision points, and emotional language of customers who downgraded or cancelled their plan in the past 90 days |
| Improve onboarding | Map the experience and language of first-time small-business owners during their first 14 days on the platform, identifying moments of confusion, success, and decision |

Translate every objective into a set of **research questions** (the things you need to learn) and a set of **discussion-guide questions** (the things you will actually ask). These are not the same. Research questions sit at the level of the project. Guide questions are the specific prompts you will use in session. Research questions belong in the proposal and the report. Guide questions belong in the moderator binder.

## Sample Frame and Sample Plan

For each segment you want to learn about, decide:

- **How many participants?** Use the rough guides in the table below.
- **What is the recruitment definition?** Behavior, role, demographic, experience. Be specific.
- **What contrasts do you need?** A study of "customers" with no segmentation usually produces mush. Splitting by tenure, behavior, channel, region, or some other meaningful axis usually produces signal.

Rough sample-size guides for commercial qual:

| Method | Per-segment floor | Common commercial practice | When to go higher |
|---|---|---|---|
| Focus groups | 2 groups per segment | 2 to 3 groups per segment | Multiple regions; sub-segments inside; polarized topic |
| Mini-groups | 2 groups per segment | 2 to 3 groups per segment | Same as above |
| IDIs | 5 to 6 per segment | 6 to 8 per segment | High variability, high-stakes decision, true discovery work, or academic-leaning rigor (go to 10 to 16) |
| Customer visits (B2B) | 8 total | 12 to 18 total | Global accounts; multiple buyer roles per account |
| Online community | 20 to 30 participants | 40 to 80 participants | Multi-week study; multiple sub-tasks |
| Diary / mobile ethnography | 8 to 10 participants | 12 to 20 participants | Multi-segment or multi-region |

These are commercial rules of thumb, not academic standards. Academic qual frequently goes higher (Creswell's grounded-theory range starts at 20 IDIs). Commercial practice has settled around the numbers above because they are usually enough to hit working saturation, which is "fresh interviews stop producing fresh codes." The honest answer to "how many is enough" is exactly that, observed in the data, not predicted in advance.

A useful pattern for studies that need to compare two or three segments: 6 to 8 IDIs per segment, total of 12 to 24. Each segment is independently usable; the contrasts across segments are where the strongest findings tend to live.

A note on B2B: in many B2B segments the true universe is small (50 to 200 named accounts in a category, say). A "sample" of 12 to 16 IDIs is a meaningful share of the population. Commercial qual in B2B routinely lands closer to a census than to a sample.

## Scaling Qual to Budget

A common beginner assumption is that qual costs $25k to $50k and is therefore out of reach. That is the standard agency-led price for a full-service project. There are leaner ways to do qual well, and beginners should know what they are.

The biggest budget levers, roughly in order:

1. **Whether you self-moderate or hire a moderator.** A professional moderator runs $150 to $400 per hour and is the single largest line item in many studies. Self-moderation cuts cost by 40 to 60 percent. It is reasonable for B2B work and for any project where the researcher knows the category. Hire a moderator when neutrality, craft, or specific expertise (kids, sensitive topics, certain languages) matters most.

2. **Recruiting channel.** Specialist recruiters ($75 to $250 per consumer recruit, $200 to $800 per B2B recruit) versus online panels (User Interviews, Respondent.io, Wynter for B2B; $50 to $300 per recruit) versus client list (effectively free, but biased). The cost ratio is often 3x to 5x.

3. **Sample size.** 6 IDIs at $150 incentive each is $900. 16 IDIs at $250 each is $4,000. Both can produce solid findings if the sample is well-defined.

4. **Transcription approach.** Professional human transcription ($1.50 to $2.50 per minute) versus AI transcription with review (low cost or free) versus AI transcription with no review (lowest cost, lowest quality, not recommended for high-stakes work).

5. **Analysis approach.** Manual coding by the researcher (free, slow) versus AI-assisted coding with manual review (faster, similar cost) versus paid CAQDAS software (NVivo, Dedoose; subscriptions).

6. **Deliverable polish.** A working memo or a Google Doc versus a polished deck. Both can be persuasive; the deck takes 5 to 15 extra hours.

Working budget tiers:

| Tier | Total cost | Typical scope | Trade-offs |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Shoestring** | $1k to $5k | 5 to 8 self-moderated online IDIs, panel-recruited, AI transcription, manual coding, memo or short doc | Time-heavy on the researcher (60 to 80 hours). Best for solo founders, internal startup research, scrappy projects with a domain-knowledgeable researcher. |
| **Modest** | $5k to $15k | 8 to 12 IDIs or 2 focus groups, panel or light agency recruit, paid AI tools for transcription and first-pass coding, polished deliverable | Balances researcher time with paid help. Workable for most small commercial projects. |
| **Standard** | $20k to $40k | 12 to 16 IDIs or 3 to 4 focus groups, professional recruiter, optional hired moderator, full transcription, formal analysis, polished deck | The default agency price. Appropriate for meaningful commercial decisions and client-facing work. |
| **Full** | $50k+ | Larger sample, multi-mode (online plus offline, or live plus async community), multi-segment or multi-country, professional team | High-stakes decisions: market entry, major launches, ongoing tracker programs. |

Where to start when budget is tight:

- **Recruit through a panel** rather than a specialist recruiter. Quality is lower but workable for most consumer and many B2B studies.
- **Self-moderate** if you have category knowledge and can stay neutral.
- **Use AI transcription** (Otter, MS Teams, Descript) with a manual review of the key passages.
- **Use AI for a first-pass scan of transcripts** to surface candidate themes, then code manually using the AI scan as a starting point. (See `analysis-and-coding.md` for the workflow.)
- **Skip the deck.** Deliver a 5- to 10-page memo or a working doc. If a client needs a deck later, build it from the memo.

A scrappy project does not have to mean a sloppy project. The integrity guardrails (informed consent, honest reporting, anonymization, no fake numbers) apply at every budget tier. The only thing that scales with budget is breadth and polish.

## Exploratory vs. Evaluative Qual

Qual studies fall along a spectrum from open exploration to focused evaluation. Beginners often run an evaluative study with an exploratory mindset or vice versa.

| Study type | Best when | Typical methods | Typical output |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Exploratory** | The problem is poorly defined, you do not know what matters | Open-ended IDIs, online communities, ethnography | Themes, vocabulary, hypotheses for further work |
| **Generative** | You are early in product or message development | Co-creation focus groups, idea-building IDIs | Concepts, language, opportunity areas |
| **Evaluative** | You have concepts, designs, or messages to test with customers | Sequential monadic IDIs, stimulus-led focus groups | Reactions, decision drivers, refinements |
| **Diagnostic** | A quant or behavioral result has surprised you and you need to know why | Targeted IDIs with affected segments | Mechanism, causal story, redesign options |
| **Longitudinal / journey** | You need to understand experience over time | Diary studies, online communities, repeat IDIs | Journey maps, moments-that-matter, evolution of attitude |

Knowing where on this spectrum you sit changes the discussion guide structure, the stimulus design, the recruiting language, and the report format. State it clearly at the start of every project.

## Pluralistic Designs (Combining Methods)

Qual and quant complement each other and qual methods complement each other. Common combinations:

- **Qual then quant.** Use exploratory qual to surface themes, vocabulary, and hypotheses. Use quant survey to size and confirm. This is the dominant pluralistic pattern.
- **Quant then qual.** Use a survey to find the surprising or interesting subgroups. Use IDIs with those subgroups to understand the why.
- **Async then live.** Use an online community or diary study to build context and vocabulary. Use live focus groups or IDIs to deepen on the topics that emerged.
- **IDI plus observation.** Pair an in-home or in-context observation with an IDI immediately after. The observation produces behavioral data; the IDI produces interpretive data.
- **Customer visits plus internal interviews.** In B2B, the customer-facing field work pairs cleanly with internal interviews (sales, customer success, product) so you can compare the inside-out view with the outside-in view.

Be deliberate about sequencing. The order changes the answers.

## What Beginners Get Wrong at the Design Stage

These are the most common, most costly design errors:

1. **Picking a focus group out of habit.** Focus groups are the default for non-researchers. They are not always the right method. Re-decide for each project.

2. **Vague objectives.** "Understand customers" is not an objective. It is a fishing license. Tighten it before recruiting.

3. **Recruiting too broadly.** A study of "consumers" answers nothing. Define the segment, the behavior, the time frame.

4. **Mixing very different participants in one group.** Heterogeneity belongs across groups, not inside a group. A group with two CFOs and six end-users is a disaster.

5. **Skipping a pilot.** If a guide cannot be piloted (focus groups), iterate it after every group instead. Even one round of revision saves the project.

6. **Treating the first interview as data.** Treat the first one or two interviews as part of pilot. Refine the guide based on what you learn.

7. **Over-stuffing the discussion guide.** Two hours of guide will not fit in 90 minutes of session. Decide the must-cover questions and accept that the rest are nice-to-have.

8. **Recruiting for the convenience of the team rather than the question.** A study of B2B buying that fails to include the legal or procurement role because they are hard to schedule will miss the actual blocker on every deal.

9. **Letting the client choose the participants.** Clients who recruit their own customers (their happiest, most articulate, most senior) get back what they put in: a flattering, biased sample. Recruit independently or, at minimum, recruit independently for a portion of the sample.

10. **Letting the client write the questions.** Clients describe what they want to know. A moderator's job is to translate that into questions a customer can actually answer. Question wording is moderator work, not client work.
