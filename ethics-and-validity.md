# Ethics and Validity

Two practices that beginners often treat as optional or as paperwork. They are neither. Ethics protect the participants, the researcher, and the credibility of the work. Validity (or, in qual terms, trustworthiness) is what separates a project a stakeholder can act on from a project they cannot.

This file covers informed consent, vulnerable populations, anonymization, the Lincoln-Guba trustworthiness criteria, reflexivity, and audit trails.

---

## Informed Consent

Informed consent means the participant agrees to the research with a clear understanding of what they are agreeing to. The standard is not "they did not object." The standard is "they understood, and they chose."

Consent has to cover, at minimum:

- **Who the researcher is.** Name, organization (or the firm conducting the work).
- **The sponsor.** When possible, name the sponsor. If the sponsor is masked for research integrity, disclose afterward.
- **The purpose of the research.** In plain language. "We are talking with people who recently signed up for [Platform X] to understand the experience."
- **What participation involves.** Duration, format, any follow-up.
- **What is being recorded.** Audio, video, screen capture, transcript. Who will have access.
- **How the data will be used.** Internal report, public publication, marketing, AI training. Each different use needs its own disclosure.
- **Confidentiality protections.** Pseudonyms, aggregation, no individual reporting back to the sponsor.
- **The right to decline any question and to withdraw at any time, without penalty.**
- **Incentive details.** How much, when paid, how delivered.
- **Contact for questions or concerns.** A real email or phone number that goes to a real person.

In writing where possible. A signed consent form (paper or digital) creates a record. For online sessions, a checkbox plus a brief verbal confirmation at the top of the session is the working norm.

For sensitive research, longer-form consent (a script the moderator reads at the start with the participant confirming each section) is appropriate.

For sponsored research where the sponsor is masked, the consent should disclose that the sponsor is not being named in this session and that the participant can ask afterward.

A note on AI: if any AI tool will be used in the research (transcription, coding, summarization), disclose it. Participants should know whether their voice goes through a third-party AI service.

A note on data storage and deletion: tell participants how long their data will be retained and what happens to it after that. The general norm is 1 to 5 years for research use, after which raw recordings are destroyed and only aggregated, anonymized findings remain.

## Vulnerable Populations

Some populations require extra care:

- **Children (under 18).** Parental consent is required as well as child assent. Format adapts (shorter sessions, age-appropriate language, observer parent if needed). For online, COPPA applies for under-13s in the US.
- **People in active medical treatment, in recovery, or with serious illness.** Confirm participation does not interfere with treatment. Be prepared for emotional content; do not push. Have referral information ready if a participant becomes distressed.
- **People in vulnerable financial situations.** Be careful that incentives do not function as coercion. Be careful with topics that touch on financial shame.
- **Prisoners, refugees, and people in coercive institutions.** Research with these populations requires institutional review and specialist expertise. Most commercial qual is not the right venue.
- **Employees of the sponsor.** Power dynamics matter. Employees may feel pressure to give the answer that pleases their employer. Anonymization protections need to be visibly real, not just on paper.
- **People in non-dominant cultural or linguistic groups.** Use a moderator who shares enough cultural context to read the room. Translate the consent form. Allow native-language responses.
- **People with cognitive or sensory differences.** Adapt format (shorter sessions, simpler language, visual aids, support persons present). Do not assume capacity.

A simple working rule: if the population needs extra care, your consent process should look different and the moderator should be trained for the specific population.

## Anonymization

The principle: a third party reading the report should not be able to identify an individual participant.

Choosing the right level of anonymization depends on how identifiable the population is. The discipline scales with the size and specificity of the market.

### For broad consumer studies

Pseudonyms work. Replace real names with assigned aliases. Use culturally consistent aliases (do not change "Bilal" to "Bob"; use another South Asian name). Attribute quotes with the pseudonym plus a brief, generic role or segment descriptor: "Bilal, retail business owner, signed up 14 days ago."

In broad consumer samples (tens of thousands or more in the population), this level is usually sufficient.

### For B2B, specialist markets, and small populations

Pseudonyms are often not enough. Use **Subject A, Subject B, Subject C** and a separate demographic table.

This applies whenever the addressable population is small enough that a combination of role, geography, specialty, and demographic detail could identify an individual. Examples:

- B2B studies in any specialist niche (enterprise software, regulated industries, named accounts)
- Medical professionals (cardiologists, neurosurgeons, oncologists in a region)
- Senior executives (CFOs of mid-cap public companies, founders in a vertical)
- Niche consumer studies (collectors, hobbyists, members of small communities)

Even initials are risky in these contexts. "Dr. M." identifies a single person if the cardiology population at three hospitals in a metro is small. The pattern to use:

In the report, attribute quotes as:

> "We need data from the European trials, not just the US ones, before I'd be comfortable recommending it." (Subject D)

Then, separately at the front or back of the report, include a demographic table:

| Subject | Role | Specialty | Setting | Years in practice |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Subject A | Interventional cardiologist | Structural heart | Academic medical centre, US Northeast | 12 |
| Subject B | Interventional cardiologist | Structural heart | Community hospital, US Midwest | 7 |
| Subject C | Cardiothoracic surgeon | Adult cardiac surgery | Academic medical centre, US South | 22 |
| Subject D | Interventional cardiologist | General | Community hospital, US West Coast | 15 |

This gives the reader enough context to interpret the quote (Subject D's view comes from a generalist in a community setting), without anyone being able to identify a specific named individual.

The demographic table is the key practice. It supports interpretation without enabling identification. Many beginners skip the table and then either over-disclose (in attempt to give context) or under-disclose (leaving the reader without enough to interpret). The table threads the needle.

### General practices that apply everywhere

- **Role and segment, not identifying detail.** "A retail business owner who signed up in the past 30 days" is anonymized. "The owner of a corner store in the east end of Toronto" may be identifiable.
- **Aggregate small categories.** If only one participant in the sample is a vegan baker, do not report that detail with quotes attributed to them. Mask the category or note the role generically.
- **Strip identifying detail from quotes.** "I work with [Specific Vendor] at [Specific Client]" becomes "I work with a vendor at a client." "I trained under [Specific Famous Professor] at [Specific Famous Program]" becomes "I trained under a senior mentor at a leading program."
- **Pixelate or omit identifying photos and video.** Visible logos, license plates, family photos, address numbers. Blur or crop.
- **Verify with a colleague.** Have someone unfamiliar with the participants read the draft and look for identifying detail. They will catch what you missed. For small B2B studies, this step is non-negotiable.

A note on internal research within a client organization: when employees of the client are the participants, anonymization needs to be even more careful than B2B external. The client likely knows who said what just from phrasing or viewpoint. Use Subject A/B/C, aggregate ruthlessly, and consider whether some findings should be reported only in aggregate without quotes at all. The client knowing who said what undermines trust for any future internal research.

## Honest Reporting

The most-violated ethical principle in commercial qual is honest reporting. Pressure to confirm the client's hypothesis, to soft-pedal critical findings, or to omit the participant who hated the concept, is real.

The discipline:

- **Report what was said, including what cuts against the client's preference.** A report that only confirms is a sales document, not a research finding.
- **Present negative cases and contrasts.** Findings stated alongside their exceptions are more credible.
- **Distinguish observation from inference.** Quote when quoting. Mark interpretation clearly.
- **Acknowledge limitations of the sample.** Who you talked to, who you did not, what is and is not addressable from the data.
- **Reframe the brief if the data does.** Sometimes the most useful finding is that the client was asking the wrong question. Say so.
- **Refuse leading directions from the client during analysis.** "Can we just focus on the positive feedback for the deck?" The answer is no.

A test: would I be willing to defend this report under deposition? The honest answer should be yes.

## Trustworthiness: The Four Lincoln-Guba Criteria

In quant, the rigor framework is validity and reliability. In qual, the standard framework (from Lincoln and Guba, 1985) is trustworthiness, which has four criteria.

### Credibility (analog: internal validity)

The findings ring true from the perspective of the participants and a knowledgeable reader. Practices that build credibility:

- **Triangulation.** Build themes from multiple sources of evidence: multiple participants, multiple data types (interviews plus diaries plus behavioral logs), multiple researchers. A finding supported by triangulation is more credible.
- **Member checking.** Take the polished findings (not raw transcripts) back to a few participants. Do these ring true? Did we get something wrong? Light-touch member checking by email or a 20-minute follow-up call is feasible for most commercial studies.
- **Prolonged engagement.** Spend enough time with the population to understand its context. In commercial work, this often means multiple sessions, multiple modes, or pairing with adjacent data (e.g., observation alongside interviews).
- **Negative case analysis.** Actively look for evidence that disconfirms the dominant pattern. Report what you find.
- **Peer debriefing.** Talk through your analysis with a colleague who is not on the project. They will spot blind spots.

### Transferability (analog: external validity / generalizability)

A reader can judge whether the findings would apply elsewhere. The researcher does not claim generalizability; the researcher provides enough context that the reader can judge. Practices:

- **Thick description.** Describe the setting, the participants, and the context in enough detail that a reader understands the conditions of the study.
- **Sample description.** Who was talked to, how recruited, why these and not others.
- **Boundary statements.** Explicitly: "These findings reflect first-time users of [Platform X] in the US, signed up in the past 30 days, who agreed to participate in research. They may not transfer to users who signed up earlier, who left the platform, or in other markets."

### Dependability (analog: reliability)

Another researcher repeating the same process would arrive at consistent findings. Practices:

- **Audit trail.** A documentation record of decisions: who was interviewed, what guide version, what changes were made and why, what codes were used.
- **Code definitions.** A codebook that defines each code, with example quotes.
- **Inter-coder check.** If two analysts code the same transcript, do they assign similar codes? In academic work, formal inter-coder reliability (kappa, percent agreement) is standard. In commercial work, a less formal check (a colleague codes 1 to 2 transcripts and the two analysts compare) is the working norm.

### Confirmability (analog: objectivity)

The findings are derived from the data, not from the researcher's prior beliefs. Practices:

- **Reflexivity.** State what you believed going into the project and how that may have shaped what you noticed. (More on this below.)
- **Audit trail again.** The trail should let a reader trace any finding back to the supporting data.
- **Raw quote evidence in the report.** Direct quotes are the most basic confirmability practice. A reader can see for themselves what was said.

In commercial work, the four criteria are rarely formally documented. The practices, however, are universal. A study that triangulates, surfaces negative cases, describes its sample thickly, keeps an audit trail, and includes reflexivity is a study that holds up under scrutiny.

## Reflexivity

Reflexivity is the discipline of acknowledging your own lens. The qual researcher is the research instrument; the instrument has biases.

Practices:

- **Pre-fieldwork reflexivity statement.** Before fielding, write a paragraph: what do I expect to find, what is my prior belief, what would surprise me. Keep it. Compare at the end.
- **In-session bracketing.** When you notice yourself reacting to a participant (delight, frustration, agreement), note it. The reaction is data about you, not about them.
- **Post-fieldwork reflexivity statement.** What did I expect, what did I find, what does the gap tell me. Include a version in the report where appropriate.
- **Reflexivity in team analysis.** When multiple researchers analyze the same data, name your prior beliefs to each other. Disagreements about interpretation often trace back to different priors.

Commercial qual rarely includes formal reflexivity statements in the deliverable. But the discipline of doing the work changes the work. The most honest reports often include a brief "what surprised us" or "what we expected versus what we found" section, which functions as light-touch reflexivity.

## Audit Trail

An audit trail is the documentation that lets someone else trace your findings back to their evidence. In commercial work, the minimum audit trail is:

- Project brief (the original objectives and questions)
- Sample plan and screener
- Discussion guide (with version history if it evolved)
- List of sessions with date, mode, and participant pseudonym
- Recordings (stored securely, with retention period)
- Transcripts
- Codebook (with definitions and example quotes)
- Memo file (in-progress thinking through the project)
- Final report
- Reflexivity notes

This trail does not need to be elaborate. A single project folder with these contents is sufficient. The point is that any finding in the report can be traced back to a code, which can be traced back to a passage in a transcript, which can be traced back to a recording.

## When the Ethics Get Hard

A few scenarios that beginners encounter and need to be ready for:

**A participant discloses something dangerous.** Self-harm intent, abuse of self or others, an illegal act. The moderator's first job is the participant's safety. Pause the research. Refer to crisis resources. Document. Consult with your team and, if appropriate, with the relevant authorities. Confidentiality has limits: physical safety is one of them.

**A participant becomes distressed during the session.** Pause. Acknowledge what is happening. Offer a break or to end the session. Do not push through.

**The client asks for verbatim transcripts.** Push back. Transcripts contain identifying detail and the participants did not consent to the client having them. Offer to share quotes selected and anonymized for the report instead. Where the client has a legitimate need (e.g., a regulator requires raw data), check the consent terms and obtain participant approval if possible.

**The client asks to recruit only happy customers.** Push back. A study of happy customers is not a study; it is a marketing testimonial collection. Distinguish the two. Either rescope (acceptable: "we will do testimonial collection") or insist on a balanced sample.

**A participant requests withdrawal after the session.** Honour it. Remove their data from the analysis. Confirm with them in writing.

**A vendor or recruiter cuts corners.** Recruiter brought in people who did not match the screener. Vendor cleaned a transcript in a way that distorts meaning. Confront, correct, and decide whether to continue with that vendor.

## Common Ethics and Validity Mistakes

1. **Treating consent as a form.** A signed form is not consent if the participant did not actually understand. Walk through the consent.

2. **Disclosing the sponsor after pre-screen but not after qualification.** Some participants would have declined if they had known the sponsor. Disclose at recruitment where possible.

3. **Anonymizing only the obvious detail.** First name removed but role plus geography plus company size identifies. Anonymize the combination.

4. **Reporting findings that contradict the data because the client wants them.** This is the single most damaging integrity violation in commercial qual.

5. **No reflexivity.** Writing as if the researcher is a neutral observer rather than an interpretive participant.

6. **No audit trail.** Six months later, a finding is challenged and the analyst cannot show how they got there.

7. **Treating member checking as a hassle.** Even a one-email member check ("Here are our top three findings, do they ring true to your experience?") is cheap and dramatically improves credibility.

8. **Skipping the negative case work.** A report without contrasts looks like a fairy tale.

9. **Quote attribution that identifies.** Especially in B2B.

10. **Storing recordings indefinitely.** Participants consented to a specific use and retention. Honour the time limit.

## A Quick Ethics and Validity Checklist

Before, during, and after the project:

- [ ] Consent obtained in writing where possible, with all required disclosures
- [ ] Sponsor disclosed where appropriate, masked only when necessary for research integrity
- [ ] Recording disclosed verbally before recording starts
- [ ] AI tool use disclosed if applicable
- [ ] Vulnerable population considerations addressed in design and moderation
- [ ] Anonymization plan agreed and applied throughout
- [ ] Triangulation across sources where possible
- [ ] Negative cases and contrasts identified and reported
- [ ] Sample described thickly enough for transferability judgment
- [ ] Audit trail (folder of project artifacts) maintained
- [ ] Codebook with definitions
- [ ] Reflexivity statement or equivalent self-awareness check
- [ ] Member check on top findings where feasible
- [ ] Recordings stored securely with a defined retention period
- [ ] Data destroyed per the retention agreement at end of life
