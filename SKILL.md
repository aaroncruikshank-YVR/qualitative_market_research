# Qualitative Market Research

## Purpose

This skill teaches and supports qualitative market research, with a focus on people who have never done it before. It covers the full workflow: deciding whether qual is the right tool, designing the study, writing the screener and discussion guide, recruiting, moderating (online or offline, individual or group), analyzing transcripts, and writing up findings that inform a real decision.

The operating principle for qualitative work is different from quant. Quant tries to measure how much. Qual tries to understand what and why. The unit of insight is a pattern of meaning, not a number. Small samples are a feature, not a bug, but they require honest scoping: qual tells you what exists, in what forms, and why it might matter. It does not tell you how many.

Beginners get into trouble in predictable places: they pick the wrong method for the question, they recruit too loosely, they write leading questions, they let the dominant talker run the room, they treat "I heard it twice" as a finding, and they put qual quotes in client decks as if they were survey results. Most of this skill is built to prevent those mistakes in advance.

This skill is part of the CTRS Market Intelligence family and shares its voice and integrity standards with the `quant-market-research` and `competitive-intelligence` skills.

## Integrity Guardrails

These apply to every task, every time. They are non-negotiable.

- Never quantify qual data with percentages. "Three of eight participants said" or "many," "several," "a few," "one" is honest. "37.5 percent of customers said" is dishonest. Qual samples are not designed to estimate population proportions.
- Distinguish what people said from what you inferred. Quote when quoting. Mark interpretation clearly.
- Always disclose recording. Get informed consent before the session starts, in writing wherever possible.
- Report findings that cut against the hypothesis or the client's preference. A qual report that only confirms what the buyer wanted to hear is a failed qual report.
- Anonymize. Pseudonyms, role descriptors, composite profiles. In B2B, individual customers are often identifiable from the smallest details, so be extra careful.
- Flag the scope of the sample at the top of any report. Who you talked to, how many, how recruited. A reader should be able to assess transferability for themselves.
- Be honest about your own lens. Reflexivity is not optional: state what you expected going in, and what surprised you.
- Do not "go native." Capture and represent views you disagree with as fairly as views you agree with.

## Workflow Router

When a task arrives, identify which phase of the qualitative research workflow it falls in. Read the matching reference file before acting. The references are deeper and more concrete than this overview.

### Phase 1: Should we even do qual? (Method fit)
**Triggers:** "should we do interviews or a survey," "what method should we use," "qual or quant," "how do we research X," scoping a new project, an ambiguous brief.
**Read:** `references/research-design.md`

The core question: do we already know what to count? If yes, run a survey. If no, run qual first to surface the right things to count. Qual is the right call when the problem is poorly defined, when the topic is sensitive or unconscious, when you need participants' own vocabulary, or when you are interpreting a surprising quant result. It is the wrong call when stakeholders need a population estimate or a precise market size.

### Phase 2: IDI or focus group? (Method choice)
**Triggers:** "should we do interviews or groups," "focus group versus interview," "how do I get this kind of info," "we want to talk to surgeons," "the topic is sensitive."
**Read:** `references/research-design.md`

Quick rule: pick IDIs when you need depth on individual decisions, when topics are sensitive or competitively confidential, or when participants are hard to schedule together. Pick focus groups when you want to hear language, see polarization, watch reactions to stimuli, or expose internal stakeholders to live customers. When in doubt, IDIs produce more distinct ideas per dollar; focus groups produce more visceral stakeholder buy-in.

### Phase 3: Online or offline? (Mode choice)
**Triggers:** "should it be in person," "Zoom group," "remote interviews," "we have participants in five countries," "platform recommendation," asking about Recollective / dscout / Discuss.io / Indeemo.
**Read:** `references/mode-specifics.md`

Online expands reach and lowers cost but loses some body language and physical-product interaction. Offline costs more, slower to field, but gives the richest behavioral and emotional signal. Synchronous video sits between the two. Asynchronous formats (online communities, video diaries, mobile ethnography) are a third mode that does not map cleanly onto IDI/FG and is useful for in-the-moment context.

### Phase 4: Sample plan and screener
**Triggers:** "who should we talk to," "how many interviews," "write a screener," "recruiting plan," "how do I find these people," sample-size questions, incidence questions.
**Read:** `references/recruitment-and-screening.md`

Define the population precisely. Build a small sample grid (segments by role, by behavior, by demographics). Write a short screener of factual yes/no or numeric questions, no lifestyle items. Over-recruit by 20 to 30 percent for groups. Reject "professional respondents" who do studies for a living.

### Phase 5: Discussion guide
**Triggers:** "draft a discussion guide," "interview guide," "what questions should we ask," "moderator guide," reviewing a draft guide.
**Read:** `references/discussion-guide.md`

Structure: warm-up, context, target topic, exercises or stimuli, wrap. Move from general to specific, familiar to remote, low-stakes to high-stakes. Put stimuli late, never up front (early stimulus exposure biases everything that follows). Limit total active probing to about 60 to 75 percent of session length to leave time for the unexpected.

### Phase 6: Moderation
**Triggers:** "how do I moderate," "how do I handle a dominator," "the group is quiet," "they keep agreeing with each other," "we have to ask about a sensitive thing," moderator briefing.
**Read:** `references/moderation-craft.md`

Universal moves: build rapport, set ground rules clearly, stay neutral, probe with open follow-ups ("tell me more about that," "what happened next," "why does that matter to you"), use silence, ladder from attribute to value, and treat projective techniques as occasional power tools, not a default.

### Phase 7: Transcription and coding
**Triggers:** uploads a transcript or recording, "analyze these interviews," "code this," "thematic analysis," "find themes," "what did people say about X," using NVivo / Dedoose / Atlas / AI-assisted analysis.
**Read:** `references/analysis-and-coding.md`

Read transcripts before coding. Build a starter codebook of around 10 codes plus an "other" bucket, drawn from the research objectives and a quick read of 2 to 3 transcripts. Code systematically with that codebook. If "other" exceeds 5 percent of passages, break out a new code and recode. Check coding consistency with a second-coder pass on at least one transcript. Refine codes into 5 to 7 finding statements. Memo your thinking as you go: the memos become the report. AI accelerates the early phases (codebook drafting, quote retrieval), but manual coding and interpretation stay with the analyst.

### Phase 8: Reporting
**Triggers:** "write up the findings," "qual report," "build the deck," "executive summary of the interviews," "topline," "verbatims."
**Read:** `references/reporting-and-insights.md`

Lead with the two or three most important discoveries. Organize the body by theme, not by question. Each theme: a clear statement, two to four verbatim quotes that illustrate, and an implication ("what this means for the decision"). Frame findings as directional. Use "many," "some," "a few," "one" rather than counts or percentages. Include a "what we did not hear" note when relevant.

### Cross-cutting: Ethics and trustworthiness
**Triggers:** "is this ethical," "informed consent," "vulnerable population," "how do we anonymize," "is this rigorous enough," peer review of your work, scrutiny from a client.
**Read:** `references/ethics-and-validity.md`

The four trustworthiness criteria (Lincoln and Guba) substitute for quant's reliability and validity: credibility, transferability, dependability, confirmability. Beginners should know what they are and the simple practices that establish each: triangulation, member checking, thick description, reflexivity, audit trails.

## Method Selection Quick Reference

When someone describes a business need, match it to the right qualitative method:

| Business question | Primary method | Secondary/supporting |
|---|---|---|
| What language do customers use about this category? | Focus groups (small, 2 to 4) | IDIs, online community |
| Why do customers choose X over Y? | IDIs (12 to 16) | Customer visits, journey interviews |
| What is the emotional experience of using this product? | IDIs with projective techniques | Ethnographic observation, video diaries |
| How does this concept land with customers? | Focus groups (3 to 4) with stimuli | Sequential monadic IDIs |
| What goes on in this professional's day? | Customer visits (B2B) or in-context IDIs | Diary studies, observation |
| What patterns exist in a community we do not yet understand? | Ethnography, online community | Group IDIs, observation |
| Why did our quant survey show this surprising result? | Targeted IDIs (8 to 12) | Small focus groups |
| What is unspoken or socially difficult to discuss? | IDIs, one-on-one | Projective techniques |
| What is the variation across regions/segments? | Multiple focus groups, one per segment | Cross-cultural IDIs |
| What journey does a customer go through over time? | Diary study, longitudinal IDIs | Online community |

## Mode Considerations at a Glance

The four-cell grid most beginners are choosing between:

| Mode | Strongest for | Weakest for | Sample logistics |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Offline IDI** | Sensitive topics, in-home/in-context observation, executive depth, sensory products | Distributed populations, fast turnaround, low budget | One participant per slot; travel; recording with consent |
| **Online IDI (video)** | Geography-spanning samples, hard-to-schedule professionals, lower cost | Sensory products, complex non-verbal signals, low-trust topics | Zoom / Teams / Webex with consent; cloud recording; screen-share for stimuli |
| **Offline focus group** | Energy, group synergy, polarized opinions, client viewing-room learning | Sensitive topics, distributed samples, time-poor participants | Facility with one-way mirror; viewing room; recruit 10 to 12, aim for 8 to 10 to show |
| **Online focus group (video)** | Distributed samples, niche populations, faster turnaround | Sensory products, observer-room dynamic | Discuss.io / Recollective / Zoom; recruit 8 to 10, aim for 6 to 8 to show; shorter sessions |

A fifth mode worth knowing: **asynchronous online community / MROC / mobile ethnography** (Recollective, dscout, Indeemo). Participants post text, photos, or short videos over days or weeks. Best for in-the-moment context that no live session can capture, journey work, and iterative concept exploration.

Detailed mode-by-mode craft is in `references/mode-specifics.md`.

## Working With Aaron's (or any beginner's) Material

When transcripts, recordings, screeners, guides, or draft reports are shared, follow this protocol:

1. **Read it first, react second.** If a transcript is uploaded, read it end to end before coding. If a guide is shared, read the whole flow before suggesting edits. Qual data and qual instruments only make sense in context.

2. **Match the working artifact to the phase.** Screener review is different from guide review is different from transcript coding. Use the relevant reference file for the right standards.

3. **Show your work.** When coding, output the code list and the supporting quote snippets so a reviewer can trace the path from raw data to claim. When analyzing, name the technique you are using (e.g., laddering, thematic analysis, framework analysis).

4. **Quotes before claims.** When writing up a finding, put the verbatim quote before the interpretation. The reader should be able to read the quote and see how you got to the claim.

5. **Plain language.** "Most participants we spoke to talked about onboarding as the moment they decided whether to stay" is honest. "Onboarding is the key driver of retention" is overclaiming from qual.

## What This Skill Does NOT Cover

- Survey design, statistical analysis, sampling math, conjoint, MaxDiff, segmentation modeling. Use the sibling `quant-market-research` skill.
- Competitive monitoring, market scanning, signal detection. Use the sibling `competitive-intelligence` skill.
- Academic research methodology with formal IRB and peer review. Some of the rigor practices here are borrowed from academic qual but the standards are commercial.
- Web and social analytics, SEO research. Different discipline.
- UX usability testing as a standalone craft. Some methods overlap (think-aloud, in-context observation) but UX has its own conventions; use a UX-specific resource for that work.
- Generative AI as a substitute for human qualitative listening. AI can help with coding, transcription, and synthesis. It does not replace a skilled moderator or an analyst's interpretive judgment.
