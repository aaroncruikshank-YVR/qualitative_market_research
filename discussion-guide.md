# Discussion Guide

The discussion guide is the moderator's working document. It sets the flow, the must-cover topics, the stimuli, and the time budget for a session. A good guide is short enough to be glanced at and structured enough to keep the moderator on track when a session goes sideways.

This file covers the universal structure (warm-up, context, target, stimuli, wrap), the differences between an IDI guide and a focus-group guide, where to put stimuli, time budgeting, worked examples, and the common mistakes beginners make.

---

## Universal Structure

Every qual session follows a roughly similar arc. The names differ; the shape is the same.

1. **Pre-session.** Consent, ground rules, recording disclosure. 3 to 5 minutes.
2. **Warm-up.** Low-stakes self-introduction. 5 to 10 minutes.
3. **Context.** What is the participant's world like? Background to the topic. 10 to 15 minutes.
4. **Target.** The core of the research. 30 to 60 minutes.
5. **Stimuli or exercises.** Reactions to concepts, ads, prototypes, or projective tasks. 10 to 20 minutes if included.
6. **Wrap.** Final thoughts, anything missed, gratitude. 5 minutes.

The flow has a deliberate shape: from broad to narrow, from familiar to remote, from low-stakes to high-stakes, from open to focused. Each section sets up the next. The session is a designed conversation, not a list of questions.

## The Pre-Session

This is housekeeping but it is also where trust is established. The moderator should:

- Thank the participant for their time and acknowledge the incentive.
- Explain the purpose of the session in general terms (without giving away the specific question).
- Disclose recording and how the data will be used.
- Disclose observers if any (one-way mirror, viewing room, client guests on Zoom).
- Set the ground rules: there are no right or wrong answers, all views are welcome, the participant can decline any question.
- Confirm the time commitment.

### Recording Disclosure (Use This as a Working Script)

Recording disclosure is the most important part of the pre-session. It must happen **before** you press record, with explicit verbal consent. For first-time moderators, here is a working script you can adapt:

> "Before we get started, I want to walk you through a few quick housekeeping items.
>
> First, I'd like to record our conversation today so I don't have to take notes the whole time and can focus on what you're saying. The recording will be used by our research team to write up the findings. Your name and any identifying details will not appear in our report. We'll refer to you using a pseudonym, and any quotes we use will be attributed only by general role or segment, like 'a first-time customer in the retail category.'
>
> The recording will be stored securely and deleted after the project ends, which is typically [N] months from now. Only our research team will have access to it.
>
> [If applicable:] We may use AI tools like Otter.ai to help with transcription. Your voice goes through their service to produce the transcript, after which it is deleted from theirs.
>
> [If applicable:] There are a few members of our project team listening in today as observers. Their job is to take notes. They will not interrupt or speak during our conversation. They are subject to the same confidentiality rules I just described.
>
> Are you comfortable with me recording today?"

Wait for an explicit verbal "yes" before pressing record. If the participant says no or seems uncertain, do not press them. You can offer to proceed without recording (and take notes by hand) or, if recording is required for the project, reschedule with a different participant.

In writing where possible: a signed consent form (paper or e-signature) or a platform checkbox the participant confirmed at recruitment. The verbal disclosure is in addition to, not instead of, the written record.

For focus groups, the disclosure is delivered to the whole group at the start. Every participant must agree. If one declines, you have to decide whether to lose that participant or to forego recording for that session.

### Other Pre-Session Items

In a focus group, also set the group ground rules: one person speaks at a time, everyone is invited to contribute, it is OK to disagree, please mute when not speaking (online), and the moderator may steer the conversation if needed.

For online sessions, add a tech check at the top: "Can everyone hear me OK? Can you see my screen if I share it?" This catches problems before they derail the session.

For sensitive topics, add a disclosure about the option to skip: "Some of the questions today may touch on personal experiences. You are welcome to skip any question or end the session at any time, and you'll still receive the full incentive."

## The Warm-Up

The warm-up is for the participant, not for you. The goal is to ease the person into the session so that by the time you ask the first real question, they are comfortable speaking.

Good warm-up moves:

- "Tell me a little about yourself: where you live, what you do, who's in your household."
- "Walk me through what a typical morning looks like."
- "What did you do last weekend?"

In a group, go around the table or screen with a low-stakes intro: first name, neighbourhood, one fact about themselves. The point is to get every voice into the room early. People who do not speak in the first 10 minutes are likely to stay quiet for the whole session.

Beginners commonly skip the warm-up to "get to the real questions." This is a mistake. The first answer a cold participant gives is rarely their best one.

## The Context Section

Before asking about the specific topic of the study, ask about the broader space the topic lives in. This does three things: it grounds you in the participant's world, it surfaces the language they use about the category, and it makes the target questions feel like a natural next step.

Examples:

- For a study about a specific software product: "Walk me through the tools you use to do [job-to-be-done] day to day."
- For a study about an ad: "What kinds of things make you trust a brand? What makes you suspicious of one?"
- For a study about a healthcare experience: "Tell me about the last time you needed to see a doctor. Walk me through what happened from the moment you decided you needed an appointment."
- For a B2B buying study: "Tell me about a recent significant purchase decision you made for the business. Not necessarily in our category, just any meaningful one. Walk me through how it unfolded."

The context section is where you usually learn the most about how participants frame the world. Pay close attention to the words they use. Borrow them in later sections.

## The Target Section

This is the core of the study. The questions here directly serve the research objectives.

Sequencing principles:

- **Move from general to specific.** Start with the broadest version of the question, then drill down. "How do you decide which X to buy?" before "Why did you choose [specific product]?"
- **Move from past to future.** Past behavior is more diagnostic than stated intent. Anchor a future question in a specific past episode.
- **Anchor in a specific instance.** "Tell me about the most recent time you [did the thing]" is more useful than "How often do you [do the thing] in general."
- **Leave stimuli out of the early target.** Pre-stimulus responses are valuable. Hear what the participant believes before you show them what you want them to react to.
- **Probe before you move on.** When a participant gives a short answer, probe before moving to the next question. "Tell me more about that." "What do you mean by [their phrase]?" "What happened next?" Three probes per question is the rough working rule.

Typical target-section moves:

- **Episode reconstruction.** "Walk me through what happened, from when you first noticed the problem to when you decided to do something about it."
- **Comparison.** "How does this compare to [adjacent product / past version / competitor]?"
- **Laddering.** "Why does that matter to you?" Then again. Then again. Three iterations gets from attribute to value.
- **Critical-incident technique.** "Tell me about a time when this worked particularly well for you. What about a time when it really did not."

In a focus group, target-section moves are similar but with group adaptations: round-robins for first reactions, written exercises (silent vote, pre-discussion writing) to capture independent views before group dynamics take over, and explicit invitations to dissent ("Has anyone had a different experience? I want to make sure we hear all sides").

## Stimuli and Exercises

Stimuli are external materials shown to participants for reaction: ads, packaging, concept statements, prototypes, screen mockups, brand statements. Exercises are participatory tasks: card sorts, ranking, projective techniques, drawing, role play.

Where to put them:

- **Late in the target section, not early.** Pre-stimulus responses are clean. Once a stimulus is shown, every later response is in some way shaped by it. Hear what the participant believes before contaminating with stimuli.
- **One at a time, in a controlled order.** If testing multiple stimuli, decide whether to rotate the order across sessions (to wash out order effects) or to anchor the order (because one stimulus is the dominant ask and the others are warm-up).
- **With clear instructions.** "I am going to show you a concept for a new [thing]. Read it once at your own pace, then tell me your first reaction. Do not worry about being polite. We want to hear honest first thoughts."
- **With a structured probe sequence.** First reaction, what stood out, what was confusing, what was attractive, what was off-putting, what was missing.

Common stimuli and exercises:

- **Concept statement.** A short written description of a product or service idea. Read aloud or silently. Probe.
- **Ad or video.** Play once. Then ask, then play again, then ask more.
- **Card sort.** Give the participant a set of cards (attributes, brands, statements) and ask them to sort by importance, preference, or category.
- **Forced trade-off.** "If you had to choose between A and B, which would you pick? Why?"
- **Word association.** "When I say [category / brand], what comes to mind?"
- **Sentence completion.** "When I see the [Brand X] logo, I feel ___."
- **Picture sort.** Set of photographs or moodboards. Pick the ones that best represent [feeling, brand, experience].
- **Projective: third-person.** "Imagine your neighbour, not you. What would she think of this?"
- **Drawing.** "Draw what your experience of [thing] looked like." Most useful for emotional or process topics.

A note on projective techniques: they are powerful when the topic is sensitive or unconscious, and weak when the topic is direct and conscious. Use them selectively. They take time and require interpretive skill. A discussion guide should not include three projective exercises by default.

## The Wrap

The wrap exists for two reasons: to catch what you missed, and to leave the participant feeling heard.

Closing moves:

- "Is there anything we did not get to today that you think is important on this topic?"
- "If you could change one thing about [the experience / the product / the category], what would it be?"
- "What is one piece of advice you would give [the client] on this topic?"
- "Anything you would like to ask me before we close?"

Then thank the participant, confirm the incentive process, and close.

In focus groups, the "false termination" is a common technique: "Thanks everyone, that's all my questions, you can grab your things." Then, as people relax, "One more thing, while we're here. What would your friends say about [X]?" Some of the most useful material comes after the official end.

## Time Budgeting

A 60-minute session has roughly 50 minutes of usable content time. A 90-minute session has roughly 75 minutes.

A guide that allots more time than that is too long. Beginners routinely write guides that contain 110 minutes of questions for a 90-minute session and then panic-rush the last third.

The fix: triage. Decide before fielding which questions are **must-cover** and which are **nice-to-have**. The must-cover questions should fit in 60 to 65 percent of the session time, leaving room for probing, tangents, and the wrap.

Mark each question in the guide with a priority. Cover priority 1s first. Get to 2s if time allows. Drop 3s without regret.

## IDI Guide vs. Focus Group Guide

The basic structure is the same. The differences:

**IDI guide:**

- All questions are individual. No round-robins, no group moves.
- Heavier on episode reconstruction. The participant has the whole session to walk through their experience.
- Probing is the engine: 3 to 5 probes per substantive question is typical.
- Total length is typically 60 to 90 minutes.

**Focus group guide:**

- Built around group moves: round-robins, paired discussions, silent writing, voting, hand-raising.
- Each major topic gets less depth per participant; more breadth across participants.
- Moderator must explicitly invite quiet voices and gently throttle dominators.
- Stimuli are typically shown and discussed as a group (with structured silent-writing first to capture pre-discussion individual views).
- Total length is typically 90 to 120 minutes.

A focus-group guide should also explicitly schedule one or two silent-writing or individual exercises. These produce independent views that survive the group dynamic.

## Worked Example: IDI Guide

For an IDI study on the small-business onboarding experience for a payments platform.

**Pre-session (3 min).** Greeting, consent, recording disclosure, ground rules, confirm time.

**Warm-up (5 min).**
- Tell me about your business. What you do, how long you've been at it, who works with you.
- What does a typical day look like for you?

**Context (10 min).**
- How does your business handle payments today? Walk me through it.
- What tools do you use for the business side of things, things like invoicing, accounting, scheduling?
- What was your business set-up like before you signed up for [Platform X]?

**Target: signup decision (15 min).**
- Walk me through how you came to choose [Platform X]. Where did you first hear about it?
- What other options were you considering?
- What was the moment you decided to sign up?
- How did you feel right before you clicked sign up?

**Target: signup experience (20 min).**
- Walk me through the signup process as you experienced it. Where did you start, what did you do next?
- Where did you get stuck, confused, or frustrated? [Probe each instance]
- Was there a point where you almost gave up? What happened? [If yes]
- What was the moment you knew the account was set up?

**Target: first 14 days (15 min).**
- After you signed up, what was the next thing you did?
- Walk me through your first transaction. What was that experience like?
- What did you have to figure out on your own? Where did you go for help?
- Looking back, what would you have wanted to know before you started?

**Wrap (5 min).**
- If you could change one thing about the signup experience, what would it be?
- Anything we did not talk about that you think is important?
- Confirm incentive process. Thank.

Total: ~73 minutes for a 90-minute booking.

## Common Discussion-Guide Mistakes

1. **Over-stuffing the guide.** 110 minutes of questions for a 90-minute session. Triage before fielding.

2. **Leading questions.** "Don't you think the new design is cleaner?" Replace with "What is your reaction to the design?"

3. **Double-barreled questions.** "How easy and clear was the onboarding?" Two questions in one. Split them.

4. **Asking for opinions instead of stories.** "What do you think of X?" produces shallow, polite answers. "Tell me about the last time you used X. What happened?" produces specifics.

5. **Hypotheticals as the main work.** "If we offered Y, would you use it?" People are terrible at predicting their own behavior. Anchor in past behavior instead, then extrapolate carefully.

6. **Stimuli too early.** Showing the ad in minute 10 contaminates everything that comes after. Stimuli go in the late middle.

7. **No probes written down.** A guide that lists topline questions but no probes leaves moderators to invent them in the moment. Write 2 to 4 probes per substantive question.

8. **Speaking the client's vocabulary in the questions.** Clients say "consideration set." Customers say "the apps I'd think about using." Translate.

9. **No room for the unexpected.** A guide so dense that there is no time for the unscripted thread. Leave 15 percent slack.

10. **Skipping the warm-up.** The warm-up is not optional. It is where the participant becomes someone who can actually be interviewed.

## Iterating the Guide

A discussion guide is never final after the first draft. It evolves as you learn.

- For IDI projects: revise after the first one or two interviews based on what you heard.
- For focus group projects: revise after each group. Even small revisions (clearer wording, dropping a question that did not work, adding a probe that did) compound across the project.
- Keep a one-line revision log so you can explain in the report what you changed and why.
