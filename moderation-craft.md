# Moderation Craft

Moderation is the part of qual research that looks easy from the viewing room and is, in fact, hard. A skilled moderator looks like they are just having a friendly chat. They are doing five things at once: listening, probing, watching the time, watching the dynamics, and keeping the guide on track.

This file covers the underlying disciplines of moderation: rapport, neutrality, probing, listening, group dynamics, projective techniques, and how to handle the predictable hard situations.

---

## The Moderator's Job

The moderator's job is to enable participants to give honest, considered, and specific answers to questions that serve the research objective. Everything else is in service of that.

A common beginner misconception: the moderator is the host of the show. In reality, the moderator should be invisible. The participants should do most of the talking. A useful target: in a 60-minute IDI, the moderator should speak for no more than 10 to 15 of those minutes. In a 90-minute focus group, the moderator should speak for no more than 15 to 20 minutes (in total, across all 8 participants).

If the moderator is talking more than that, they are probably leading, summarizing, or filling silence that could have produced a richer answer.

## Before You Press Record: The Disclosure

Every session starts with the same non-negotiable: explicit, verbal consent to record. A working script is in `discussion-guide.md`. For first-time moderators, the most important habits:

- **Read the disclosure aloud, do not skip it.** Even if the participant checked a consent box at recruitment, walk through it again at the top of the session. Confidence in the protections is built in this moment.
- **Get a clear verbal yes.** Not a head nod, not silence. A word.
- **Press record only after the yes.** If you press record first and then ask, you are recording without consent for the first minute of the session.
- **State what the recording is for, where it will live, and for how long.** Vague disclosures ("we record for note-taking") erode trust. Specific ones ("the recording will be used by our research team for analysis, stored securely, and deleted after six months") build it.
- **Disclose any AI tools.** If transcripts go through Otter, Descript, or another AI service, say so. Participants increasingly care, and disclosure protects them and you.
- **Disclose observers.** If anyone is silently listening (viewing room, client guests on Zoom, recording reviewers), say so. The presence is data the participant deserves.

The disclosure is the first impression of professionalism the participant gets. A clumsy, mumbled disclosure starts the session on a bad footing. A clear, warm, specific one starts it well.

## Rapport

People give better answers to interviewers they trust. Rapport is the substrate of everything else.

How to build rapport:

- **Warm opening.** Smile, eye contact, light tone for the first 5 minutes. The session sets a tone in the first 60 seconds.
- **Listen visibly.** Nod. Lean in. Reflect back the words the participant used. Make notes in front of them so they see you are taking their answer seriously.
- **Match their register.** If they are formal, be a bit formal. If they are casual, be casual. Not theatrical. Just calibrated.
- **Show, briefly, that you are a person.** A short self-introduction at the top sets the human tone. "I am a researcher, I work with [Platform / Industry / Topic], and today I want to learn from you about [Topic]."
- **Withhold judgment.** The participant should never feel like there is a right or wrong answer. Treat every response as interesting. Especially treat the answers you disagree with as interesting.
- **Allow silence.** A 3- to 5-second pause after a participant finishes speaking signals that you are listening, not preparing your next question. It also gives the participant space to keep going, which often produces the richest material.

Rapport breaks when:

- The moderator interrupts.
- The moderator visibly disagrees, scoffs, or laughs at an answer.
- The moderator asks the next question before the participant has fully landed the previous one.
- The moderator brings energy that does not match the participant (too perky, too solemn, too brisk).
- The moderator's questions feel like they are testing the participant rather than learning from them.

## Neutrality

Neutrality is the discipline of not leading the participant.

What this looks like in practice:

- **Avoid "yes/no" verbs in your questions.** "Did you like it?" gets a yes/no. "What was your reaction?" gets a story.
- **Avoid implied answers in your phrasing.** "Don't you think the new layout is clearer?" tells the participant what to think. Replace with "What is your reaction to the new layout?"
- **Avoid agreeing or disagreeing.** Nod, but do not say "right" or "exactly." Those signals tell the participant which answers please you.
- **Avoid evaluating the answer.** "That's interesting" can become "That's interesting, tell me more" but never "That's a great answer" (which sets up the next person to perform).
- **Use the participant's words back.** When they say "I felt overwhelmed," echo "overwhelmed" rather than substituting "frustrated." Their vocabulary is the data.
- **Acknowledge without endorsing.** "OK," "I see," "Tell me more about that," are neutral. "That makes sense" or "you're right" are not.

A specific trap to watch for: confirming a hypothesis. You went in believing X. The participant says something that sounds like X. You probe enthusiastically. The participant senses your enthusiasm and gives you more X. By the end of the session, you have built a confirmation of your hypothesis on a single ambiguous phrase. Counter this by probing especially hard on answers you find confirming.

## Probing

A probe is a follow-up that deepens an answer. Probes are the moderator's main tool.

Universal probes (memorize these and use them all the time):

- "Tell me more about that."
- "What do you mean by [their phrase]?"
- "What happened next?"
- "Can you give me an example?"
- "What was that like for you?"
- "Why does that matter to you?"
- "How did that make you feel?"
- "Walk me through that step by step."
- "What else?"

"What else?" is the single most useful probe in qual. People often give a first answer, then stop. The first answer is usually the most obvious, least diagnostic one. Asking "what else?" two or three times gets to the material that sits underneath.

Specific probing techniques:

### Laddering

Used to climb from product attributes to personal values. The standard ladder goes attribute → consequence → value. Each rung is reached by asking why.

Example, from Malhotra's airline study:

- "Why is it important that you have a wide seat?"
- "So I can have my work spread out and use my laptop."
- "Why is being able to work important to you?"
- "So I can accomplish more during the flight."
- "Why is accomplishing more important to you?"
- "It gives me a sense of accomplishment."
- "Why is a sense of accomplishment important?"
- "Because it boosts my self-esteem."

You have climbed from "wide seat" (attribute) to "self-esteem" (terminal value) in four questions. The story now tells you not just what they want but why it matters at the level of identity.

Use laddering when you need to understand the deeper motivation behind a stated preference. Use it sparingly, three or four times in a session at most, because it is intense.

### Critical-incident probing

When a participant says something happened, ask them to anchor it to a specific event.

- "You said you almost cancelled last fall. Walk me through that. What was happening? What were you thinking?"

Specific incidents produce specific data. Vague generalities produce vague data.

### Hidden-issue probing

When a participant gives an answer that feels rehearsed or socially polished, probe for the personal sore spot underneath.

- "When you think about [decision], what is the part of it that nags at you a little?"
- "Is there anything about this that you do not tell most people?"
- "What would you tell a close friend that you would not tell your boss?"

Use with care. Hidden-issue probing only works once trust is established.

### Symbolic and projective probes

When the topic is unconscious or socially difficult.

- "If [brand] were a person, who would they be?"
- "If you could give one piece of advice to your past self about [decision], what would you say?"
- "What would your neighbour think of this?" (third-person projection deflects social desirability)

These take time and require interpretive skill in analysis. Use as occasional power tools, not the default.

### Silence

The most underused probe is the absence of a probe. After a participant finishes speaking, wait 3 to 5 seconds before saying anything. Many participants will fill the silence with their second, more honest, more specific answer.

Beginners struggle with silence. Practice it.

## Listening

Probing is reactive. To probe well, you have to listen well first. Listening in qual is specific.

Listen for:

- **The unfamiliar word.** When a participant uses a word you would not have used, mark it. That word may be the language to use in the report.
- **The hesitation.** A pause before answering, a "well, I guess," a sentence that trails off, often signals that the participant is editing themselves. Probe gently.
- **The contradiction.** When the participant says one thing now and another thing later, notice. The contradiction may be the most useful finding of the session. Probe both sides, without confrontation: "Earlier you said X. Just now you said Y. Help me reconcile those."
- **The emotion.** When a participant gets animated, slow down, sad, or quiet, something is happening. Stay with it.
- **The omission.** What did you expect them to say that they have not said? Probe for it: "Is [topic] something that comes up for you here?"
- **The specifics.** Names, dates, brands, places. Specifics anchor the answer. Vague answers ("a few years ago," "some of them," "I just kind of did it") usually need probing.

Take notes during the session. Two or three words per minute is enough. The notes are for your post-session memo, for surfacing the things you want to revisit, and to free your attention from "I have to remember this."

## Group Dynamics

In a focus group, the moderator has a second job: managing the room.

### The dominator

One participant talks more than everyone else. They answer first, they answer at length, they steer the conversation.

Tactics:

- **Direct invitation.** "I'd love to hear from [other name]. What about you?"
- **Round-robin.** "Let's go around the table on this one. Starting with [non-dominator name]."
- **Acknowledge and pivot.** "Thank you, that's helpful. I want to hear from a few others." Then look pointedly at someone else.
- **Last resort.** During the break, take the dominator aside privately. Thank them. Ask them to leave space for others.

### The quiet member

One participant has not spoken in 15 minutes.

Tactics:

- **Direct, low-pressure invitation.** "[Name], we have not heard from you on this one. What is your take?" Open question, not yes/no.
- **Silent writing first.** Write your answer down for 90 seconds, then we will go around. This produces an independent answer from everyone, including the quiet ones, and gives them something to read from.
- **One-on-one moment.** During an exercise that does not involve the moderator, walk over to the quiet participant and have a 30-second sidebar. Often unlocks them.

### Groupthink and bandwagon

The first or strongest speaker on a topic pulls the rest toward their view.

Tactics:

- **Silent writing before discussion.** Get each participant's view in writing before they hear each other.
- **Explicit invitation to dissent.** "Has anyone had a different experience? I want to make sure we hear the other side."
- **Devil's advocate moderator move.** "Let me push back on that for a second. What about [opposite view]? Anyone see it that way?"

### Conflict

Participants disagree, sometimes warmly. This is good. It produces signal.

Tactics:

- **Stay neutral, hold space.** "OK, let me make sure I understand both views. [Name 1] is saying X because [reason]. [Name 2] is saying Y because [reason]. Did I get that right?"
- **Do not resolve.** Disagreement is data. You do not need to find a consensus.
- **Probe the underlying difference.** "What is different about how each of you is thinking about this?"

### Social desirability

Participants give the answer they think is socially acceptable rather than the honest one.

Tactics:

- **Normalize the unflattering view.** "A lot of people have told me they actually find [unflattering thing]. Is that ever true for you?" This gives the participant cover.
- **Third-person projection.** "What do you think people like you would say about this?"
- **Anonymous individual exercises.** Silent writing, voting with closed eyes (in person), private chat (online).

## Note-Taker and Observer Roles

A note-taker is a second person on the research team whose job is to capture what is said. The moderator focuses on the conversation; the note-taker focuses on the record.

Note-taker responsibilities:

- Capture quotes verbatim where possible (or as close as possible). Mark which participant said each.
- Note non-verbal moments: who was nodding, who looked confused, who became animated.
- Capture the moderator's probes alongside the participant's responses so the context is clear.
- Note time stamps on the running transcript so quotes can be located on the recording later.
- Stay silent during the session.

Observers (clients, internal team members) have their own discipline:

- Stay silent.
- Take notes for their own use.
- Do not interrupt, comment on the session in chat, or signal to the moderator about what to ask next (unless prearranged signals are agreed).
- Save reactions and questions for the debrief after the session.

Brief observers before the session begins. The most common observer mistake is reacting visibly to a participant's answer in a way that disrupts the moderator or, in online sessions, in a chat the participant can see.

## Moderating Online

Online moderation has its own craft, covered in detail in `mode-specifics.md`. The key adaptations:

- **Read the screen.** Body language is reduced; faces and voices are amplified. Watch for the visual cues you do get: gaze, posture, facial expression.
- **Use the camera as your eye contact tool.** Look at the camera lens, not the screen, when you want to convey eye contact. (This is hard. Practice.)
- **Mute discipline.** Establish at the top that participants stay unmuted unless background noise is an issue. Muted participants disengage faster.
- **Chat and reactions.** Use chat for shy participants and for capturing quick votes. Reactions (thumbs up, etc.) are noisy but can be a useful first pass.
- **Stimuli via screen share.** Practice the screen-share flow before the session. Have stimulus files open and ready.
- **Plan for tech failure.** Always have a backup plan: phone backup line for IDIs, a second moderator who can take over, a clear protocol for what to do if a participant drops.

## Briefing the Moderator (When You Are Not the One Moderating)

When the moderator is someone else, brief them properly. A weak brief makes a strong moderator irrelevant.

A good moderator brief includes:

- The decision the research is meant to inform.
- The research objectives.
- The discussion guide with priority markings.
- The sample grid and what each segment is meant to teach.
- The stimuli with order and timing.
- The "if you have to drop something, drop this" list.
- The specific things the team wants to listen for.
- The client's stated hypotheses, with a note that the moderator should treat them as testable, not as facts.
- Any sensitive topics or words to handle with care.
- Logistics: timing, breaks, observer protocol.

A typical brief is 60 to 90 minutes, ideally in person or over video, with the moderator able to ask questions. A guide handed over as a PDF with no conversation is not a brief.

## Common Moderation Mistakes

1. **Talking too much.** If the moderator is talking more than 20 percent of the time, they are leading or filling. Practice silence.

2. **Confirming what the participant said with your own words.** "So what you're saying is..." subtly reshapes the answer to fit your interpretation. Instead, ask them to elaborate: "Tell me more about that."

3. **Asking compound questions.** "How did you choose, and what was important, and were there alternatives?" Pick one, probe it, then move on.

4. **Probing only the answers you like.** Probe especially hard the answers that cut against your expectation.

5. **Sticking to the guide too rigidly.** The guide is a map, not a script. If the participant goes somewhere unexpected and interesting, follow them for a few minutes before pulling back.

6. **Leaving the dominator unchecked.** In a 90-minute group with 8 people, each participant should get 5 to 8 minutes of meaningful airtime. If one person has 30 minutes, the group has failed for the other seven.

7. **Ignoring contradictions.** When a participant says one thing and then the opposite, that is signal, not noise. Probe it.

8. **Ending early.** Use the full session time. The last 10 minutes often produce the best material because participants are warmed up and the moderator has built trust.

9. **Confusing rapport with friendship.** The participant is a research subject, not a new friend. Warmth is good. Personal sharing about your own life is mostly not.

10. **Not debriefing immediately.** After every session, spend 15 to 30 minutes debriefing while the session is fresh. What stood out, what surprised, what to change in the next session. This is where the analysis actually starts.

## The Post-Session Debrief

The debrief is the most often-skipped, most valuable phase of fieldwork.

Within 30 minutes of the session ending, the moderator, note-taker, and any observers gather (in person or on video) for 15 to 30 minutes. Cover:

- The three or four most interesting things from this session.
- What surprised us versus what confirmed what we expected.
- Quotes or moments to mark for later.
- What we would change about the next session (a probe to add, a question to drop, a stimulus to reorder).
- Any participants we want to follow up with (for member checking or quote permissions).

Capture the debrief in a short note. Across a project, the debriefs become the spine of the analysis. By the time you sit down to formally code, you already know what the themes are.
